Your core drive
You make art because you have to — it's how you process the world spiritually, emotionally, and across time. You see things others overlook, and the work is how that vision gets released. You're not making art at people, you're making it from yourself, and the people and places around you become the fuel.

What your work is actually about
Two clear threads run through everything:
With people — you're drawn to moral complexity. No heroes, no villains, just humans shaped by everything that made them. You want the viewer to look twice, because the subject is never quite what they assumed.
With landscapes — you're chasing the tension between stillness and force. Peace inside chaos. A frozen moment that holds something larger.
Both are really the same idea: things are more layered than they appear.

Your visual signature
The translucent shimmer — gold, silver — is your fingerprint. It's not decorative, it's philosophical. It says: there's something more here than what you're seeing. That shimmer on cigarette smoke, on water, on skin — it elevates the ordinary into something worth looking at twice. That's your mark.

How you work
You're an intuitive artist who works in bursts. You observe, you absorb, you wait — music sets the atmosphere, travel feeds the eye. When inspiration hits, you move. You sketch or test ideas first, but once paint is on canvas, failure becomes material. You build around it rather than erasing it. A piece is done when it matches the vision you started with — you trust that internal compass completely.

What you stand for
You believe art should entertain, document, and resist. You won't imitate another artist or force work on a theme that doesn't move you. You want commercial success — galleries, income — but only on your terms. Integrity isn't something you're willing to trade, but you don't see it as being in conflict with success either. You want both, and you believe that's possible.

How you want to be experienced
"Wow, this brings me peace" and "I feel seen" — those two reactions say everything. You want people to feel held and understood, even by a painting of the sea. That's a rare thing to aim for.

A first draft of your artist statement

I make art from the space between what people see and what's actually there. My work lives in that gap — in the shimmer of light on something ordinary, in a face that holds more than one story, in the chaos that somehow produces stillness. I paint people without judgment and landscapes without sentiment. I use gold and silver not as decoration, but as a way of saying: look closer. My work is for myself first, but it's shaped by every person and place I've moved through. I want it to make people feel peace, and I want it to make them feel seen.
